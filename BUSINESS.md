## 1. The Business Manifesto

We are a **restoration collective and interactive-fiction studio** — we
lovingly restore the classic adventures we grew up on and showcase the
playthroughs on YouTube/Twitch; and we forge our own original text adventures
for a generation raised on chat interfaces. The whole world is talking to text
boxes now. We make the text box *worth* talking to — and we make sure the great
ones aren't forgotten. (We celebrate the classics on screen; we don't
distribute them — they belong to their makers.)

Six operating principles (on-brand, but they're also just good business):

1. **Two tracks, one conscience.** *Restorations* are non-commercial homage —
   we restore lost classics and showcase the playthroughs (YouTube/Twitch),
   never distribute or sell the games. *Originals* are IP we own outright and
   are free to monetize. Keep the two tracks clean and never blur them (don't
   sell, gate, or merch a restoration).
2. **The funnel is free; the deluxe is paid.** Free browser games are marketing.
   Premium episodes, collections, and merch are revenue.
3. **Own the whole stack.** Our engine, our domain, our mailing list, our art.
   "No landlords" means no platform that can deplatform us, and no investor who
   owns our roadmap.
4. **Homage, never infringement.** Inspiration is free. Trademarks are not.
5. **Small and profitable beats big and burning.** A few thousand true fans who
   buy and subscribe is a real business. We are not chasing a unicorn.
6. **The collective is literal.** Members fund the label and shape what we make.
   "From each according to their imagination" is a patronage model with a
   conscience — and patronage models (Patreon, Choice of Games, itch) are how
   actual IF studios survive.

## 2. The Careful Part — IP & Copyright (READ THIS FIRST)

Infocom and Zork are owned by **Activision** (which bought Infocom in 1986).
That ownership is live and real. Here's the plain-English risk map.

### The restoration track — read this twice 🔴
Our headline act is restoring *Return to Zork* (Activision's game). That is a
**fan restoration of someone else's copyrighted work** — a different and, if
mishandled, *bigger* risk than our original IF. The rules that keep it survivable:
- **Strictly non-commercial.** No selling the restored game, no ads on it, no
  paywall, no merch *of it*, no donations *tied to it*. The moment money touches
  a restoration, "homage" stops being a defense.
- **Ship the engine/tools, not the assets.** Follow the ScummVM model: the
  player must bring their own original game data. We distribute our *engine
  fork, patches, and remaster recipe* — not Activision's copyrighted media.
- **Showcase, don't host.** A YouTube/Twitch *showcase* of your own restoration
  is far safer than redistributing the playable game or its ripped assets.
- **Credit + disclaim.** "Return to Zork © Activision; a non-commercial fan
  restoration; no affiliation or endorsement," prominently.
- All the *paid* tiers below (originals, merch, membership) must be built on
  **our own IP**, never on a restoration. Keep the firewall absolute.

### What is genuinely risky 🔴
- **Using "ZORK" or "INFOCOM" as a product name or brand.** These are active
  trademarks. Selling anything branded with them invites a cease-and-desist.
- **Verbatim Infocom text.** The exact line *"It is pitch black. You are likely
  to be eaten by a grue."* is copyrighted expression. (Good news: the live site
  already transforms it to *"expropriated by a grue"* — keep transforming.)
- **The brand name "INFOCOMMIE" itself.** It is close to "Infocom." A trademark
  lawyer needs to weigh in on *likelihood of confusion* and *dilution*. Parody
  protects *expressive works* (the writing in a game), but it is a **weaker
  shield for a company name and for merch you sell.** This is the single biggest
  open question in the whole plan — resolve it before investing.
- **Our current catalog teasers.** `THE WINTER PALACE OF ZORK`, `A MIND FOREVER
  UNIONIZING` (a direct riff on Infocom's *A Mind Forever Voyaging*), and
  `PLUNDERED HEARTS, REDISTRIBUTED` (*Plundered Hearts* is a real Infocom game)
  all lean on Activision's marks. **These should become original titles** before
  anything is sold. (I can swap them in 5 minutes — see §7.)

### What is reasonably safe 🟢
- **Ideas, genres, and mechanics.** "A text adventure where a monster eats you
  in the dark" is an *idea* — ideas aren't copyrightable. Only specific
  expression is.
- **The craft & tradition.** The parser/IF format, the Z-machine lineage, tools
  like Inform 7 — this is an open creative tradition you're free to build in.
- **The word "grue."** It predates Zork — coined by **Jack Vance** in *The Dying
  Earth* (1950); Dave Lebling borrowed it. It's also drifted toward generic use
  in gaming culture. *But* the **specific Zork grue** (lurks in dark, eats the
  unlit) is Infocom's expression, so don't copy their wording or art — write
  your own. Safer still: **invent your own signature monster** you own 100% and
  let the grue be a one-line wink.
- **Parody, done as actual expression.** Satire and commentary inside a story
  have real protection. Just don't let parody become the *brand you bank on.*

### Action items (do these before monetizing)
1. **Trademark clearance:** search the USPTO database (TESS / `tmsearch.uspto.gov`)
   for "INFOCOMMIE" and look at the "Infocom"/"Zork" registrations Activision
   holds. Then a **1-hour consult** with a trademark attorney for a go / no-go on
   the name. Budget ~$200–500.
2. **Build original signature IP:** one monster, one world, one mascot you own
   outright. This becomes the thing you actually sell and license.
3. **Scrub the catalog** of real Activision titles/marks (§7).
4. **Keep a Plan-B name** in your back pocket in case the lawyer says "don't."
   (You can keep `infocommie.com` as a *fan homage/landing* even if the
   commercial entity ships under a cleaner trademark.)

> ⚖️ **Disclaimer:** none of the above is legal advice. It's a founder's risk
> map. Get a professional opinion before spending money on the brand.

## 6. Honest Money Math

Interactive fiction is a **niche with modest margins** — go in clear-eyed:

- **itch.io IF games** typically earn modest amounts; a good one might do
  hundreds, a breakout low thousands. Treat early games as **brand + list
  building**, not rent money.
- **Steam** is where narrative games can actually earn — a well-reviewed text-led
  game with a hook can do real numbers, but it needs polish and a store presence.
- **Membership is the steady engine:** 200 members × $5/mo ≈ **$1,000/mo
  recurring.** That's a realistic, motivating 12-month target and it compounds.
- **Merch is the surprise winner for an aesthetic this strong:** a $25 poster at
  print-on-demand nets ~$8–12 with zero inventory risk. The propaganda art is
  the product as much as the games are.

**The realistic shape of success:** not one big payday — a **stack of small,
durable streams** (members + merch + a back-catalog of cheap paid games + the
odd commission) that together pay for your time and fund the next story.

## 8. The Brand-Name Decision

Everything hinges on one fork:

- **Plan A — Keep INFOCOMMIE.** Best brand, best joke, you own the domain. But it
  *must* survive a trademark review first.
- **Plan B — Clean trademark, INFOCOMMIE as homage.** Ship the commercial label
  under an original, clearable name; keep `infocommie.com` as the beloved
  fan-homage front door and funnel. You lose nothing emotionally and gain a
  defensible asset.

**Recommendation:** pursue Plan A, but commission the legal opinion *before* you
print a single shirt or take a single dollar — and keep a Plan-B name ready. Own
what you sell; borrow only the spirit.
